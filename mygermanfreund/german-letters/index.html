German letters explained

Received a German letter you do not understand?

That moment can be stressful. The letter may look urgent, the language may feel formal, and it may not be obvious whether you need to do something today or simply keep it for your records.

This guide helps you understand how official letters in Germany are usually structured and what to check first.

Start with these five details

1

Who sent it?

Look for the name of the authority, insurance provider, bank, employer, school, landlord, or other organisation.

2

Why did they write?

The subject line and first paragraphs usually tell you whether the letter contains a request, decision, reminder, appointment, or general information.

3

Is there a deadline?

Check for dates and words such as Frist, bis zum, innerhalb, or Termin. Do not rely on memory—write the date down.

4

Do they need something from you?

Look for requested documents, signatures, payments, forms, appointments, or instructions to contact someone.

5

Where can you ask questions?

Most letters include a reference number, contact person, phone number, postal address, or online portal.

Common types of German letters

Government and local authorities

These may concern registration, residence, benefits, fees, appointments, or requested documents.

Health insurance

These often relate to membership, contributions, requested information, reimbursement, or changes to your coverage.

Tax office

Letters from the Finanzamt may include a tax number, request for information, assessment, payment notice, or deadline.

Housing and utilities

Landlords, property managers, electricity providers, and other services may write about costs, contracts, meter readings, repairs, or changes.

Schools and childcare

These can contain appointments, forms, permission requests, payment information, or messages about your child.

Employment and social insurance

Employers, pension insurance, employment agencies, and other organisations may request documents or explain a decision.

Do not ignore a letter just because it is difficult to understand.

Some letters are only informational. Others contain deadlines, payment requests, appointments, or legal consequences. Keep the original document and verify important details directly with the sender or a qualified professional when necessary.
